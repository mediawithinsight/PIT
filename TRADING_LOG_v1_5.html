1. Core framework

Catalyst timing is the primary decision layer. Entries and exits are decided by catalysts, filings, macro, and fundamentals. Nothing else gets promoted to a decision.

Everything else is a review trigger. ATR breaches, volume states, relative strength, range position — these tell you where to look. They never tell you what to do.

  • ATR stop breach → go look, not sell
  • Volume SURGE → go look, not buy
  • Range at 15% → go look, not cheap

The failure mode is leaping from signal to trade. Flagged explicitly as something to be challenged on.

2. Principles established

2.1 A 100% hit rate reprices expectations

A sustained drill hit rate means the market has already repriced upward. The next miss drops harder than the next hit lifts. Asymmetry builds silently during a winning streak.

Second-order: the better the run, the more fragile the position — not less.

2.2 Range position is a question generator, not a signal

Where price sits in its 20-day range means nothing on its own. The mean-reversion read ("low = good, high = bad") is first-order and is wrong for a catalyst-driven trader.

The real question: does the range position agree or disagree with what the catalyst timeline says should be happening right now?

  • Price at 20-day high + volume building → accumulation ahead of a catalyst, or a move already missed. Which?
  • Price at 20-day low + fully-funded program → not a funding fear. So what is it?

Worked example (14 Jul 2026): - RDU entry set at $0.160 = exactly the 20-day high (100% of range). Entry set at the top of the range. - KFR at 15% of range, filings confirm 2026 program explicitly fully funded. Cheap — or the market knows something not yet priced in.

Open: neither has been checked against the catalyst timeline. That check is the missing step.

2.3 A plan whose stop fires before its entry is not a plan

If the trailing stop sits above the level you intend to add at, you would be stopped out before the entry is ever reached. The plan contradicts itself.

Live examples (14 Jul 2026): | | Add at | Trailing stop | Price | |---|---|---|---| | ANDC | $1.05 | $1.228 | $1.170 | | RAK | $0.140 | $0.161 | $0.145 |

Both flagged by the tool. Neither resolved.

2.4 Core-locking changes what a stop breach means

ANDC and RAK are fully core-locked — meaning they will not be sold regardless of the stop.

Open question, unresolved: what does an ATR breach on a fully core-locked position actually tell you? Is it still information, or is it noise you have already decided to ignore? If the latter, should the tool still show it?

2.5 Three independent signals converging is the framework working

Trade: 14 Jul 2026 — SOLD CRCL 36,000 @ $0.150 (RRSP). Proceeds $5,400. Realized +$360. Remaining: 25,000 shares (11,000 core-locked). Avg cost $0.140.

The decision came from three signals that agreed, none of which was a price signal:

Layer What it said
Position Intelligence (PIT) Far too overweight for a name being swing traded, not held
Volume FADING — 7,000 vs 137,787 avg. 5% of normal. No liquidity to exit into later.
Filings <1 quarter runway · Phase II unfunded · financing near · Yuma King is a lease, not ownership

The principle: conviction should come from independent layers agreeing, not from one layer shouting. A chart signal alone is one layer. Position size + volume + balance sheet is three — and they were saying the same thing.

The uncomfortable part, for honesty: the exit was at $0.150, which was 100% of the 20-day range ($0.120–$0.150). The execution was excellent. But was it timed, or was it luck that the overweight/volume/financing conclusion happened to land on a local high? Worth being honest about which — because only one of those is repeatable.

Second-order note: the position was reduced but not closed, and 11,000 remain core-locked. If the financing thesis is right, the remaining 25,000 face the same dilution. Why does a name you consider too risky to hold at size, and are swing trading rather than holding, have a core lock on it at all? Unresolved.

2.6 Warrant overhang is a velocity cap

When a stock rallies, its own in-the-money warrants become exercisable supply into that rally. The rally creates the very selling that caps it.

Consequence: a name with heavy ITM warrant overhang does not move the way a clean one does, even if the news is identical. CGNT carries the largest overhang in the basket. RAK carries zero warrants on fully staked properties.

Second-order: this reorders the basket independently of geology. Two identical drill results produce two different price responses depending on the cap table.

2.7 Correlated binaries — the basket is less diversified than it looks

ANDC, KFR and BIG all print within ~6 weeks of each other.

A miss on any one sours sentiment on the others regardless of geology. Junior mining sentiment is not name-specific; a bad print in the sector drains the bid across it.

This means the basket carries a hidden concentration — not in commodity or jurisdiction, but in timing. Ten tickers is not ten independent bets when three of them resolve in the same window into the same audience.

Question worth sitting with: is the position sizing on ANDC/KFR/BIG set as if they were independent? If so, the real exposure is larger than the allocation bars suggest.

2.8 Balance-sheet quality is the hidden ranking

Cash + runway + warrant overhang + going-concern level often reorder a basket more than geology does.

Established from the filings work: | Ticker | Finding | |---|---| | RAK | No going-concern qualification · zero warrants · fully staked properties | | KFR | 2026 program explicitly fully funded per filing | | CRCL | <1 quarter runway · Yuma King is a lease, not ownership | | CGNT | Largest warrant overhang in the basket | | OGW | The only ticker carrying debt |

The principle: good news does not pay the bills. A great hole in a company with one quarter of runway means a financing at a price the market sets, not you. (This is precisely what drove the CRCL trim — §2.5.)

2.10 Core is additive — you do not swing inside a high-conviction position

Established on the 14 Jul drive, working through ANDC (avg cost ~47¢, trading ~$1.17, thesis $2.00).

The trap of swinging inside the core: sell at $1.20, buy back at $1.10, and you have captured 10¢ — but you have also ratcheted your average cost up and taken on execution risk. Do it a few times and the cost-basis creep eats the captured pennies. And if the stock rips $1.17 → $1.90 without giving you the dip, you miss the entire move while sitting in cash waiting to re-buy.

The maths only works if you need a small swing. At high conviction (thesis worth ~60¢ of upside), you would need five or six perfect cycles to match simply holding — and one mistimed exit erases the edge. So:

At high conviction, small swings inside the core lose to holding. Core is where you add on thesis-aligned dips, not where you trade volatility.

The trim exception: if a core name spikes well ahead of its next catalyst (e.g. $1.50 on news with nothing due for weeks), trimming the spike is position-size management, not swing trading. The test: are you selling to manage size, or to capture a wiggle? Only the first belongs in the core account.

2.11 The expensive lesson — selling out of fear, named

ANDC, past: held ~15,000–18,000 shares at 65¢. It ran to $1.00, fell back toward cost, and — following a self-imposed "never lose money on a trade" rule — sold near breakeven. It then ripped back up.

Cost basis today is ~99¢ instead of the 65¢ it would have been. The sale was driven by fear of a further drop, not by any change in the thesis. That is the exact failure mode §2.10 and §1.6 (RRSP-core) are built to prevent.

High conviction means you live with the dips. A stop that fires on price alone, on a position whose thesis is intact, is selling the thesis to avoid discomfort. If the conviction is real, the dip is an add, not an exit.

2.13 Buying when the technicals disagree with the thesis — FEO

15 Jul 2026: bought Oceanic (FEO) RRSP 8,000 @ $0.65 — the first trade taken off a watch tab, researched in PIT before buying (the workflow J built the tool to enforce).

Every momentum signal was against the entry: | Signal | Reading | What it usually says | |---|---|---| | 20-day range position | 0% (bottom of 0.63–0.88) | "falling knife" | | Relative strength | −7.5% (worst in basket) | "lagging the sector" | | 20-day change | −21.3% | "downtrend" | | Volume | BUILDING, trend 3.38× | heavy participation at the low — capitulation or accumulation |

This is not a contradiction to resolve — it's the framework working. Per the core rule (§2.2, and the ATR/RS-are-review-triggers principle), momentum signals are questions, not decisions. The decision rests on the thesis: FEO has the strongest balance sheet in the basket ($50.6M cash, recapitalized, no going concern), trades below the $0.75 raise price, and its re-rate catalyst is a developer-timeline PFS. That is a value/position entry, not a momentum swing — so weak momentum is expected, even desirable (you're buying what the sector is discarding).

The discipline it demonstrates: the tool made J look at the disagreement before buying instead of discovering it after. The signals didn't veto the trade; they defined what has to be true for it to work — the balance sheet buys time for the catalyst, and the entry only fails if the PFS/partner catalyst never comes. By §1.6 this is an RRSP-core / position hold, not a TFSA swing — consistent with where it landed.

Second-order watch: the BUILDING volume (3.38× trend) at the low is the one signal that could be information rather than noise. Heavy volume into a bottom is either capitulation (sellers exhausting) or distribution (informed selling). Which one resolves over the next few weeks is worth watching — not to trade on, but to confirm the thesis isn't missing something the tape knows.

3. Data integrity as a trading discipline

The recurring theme across every session: the danger is never wrong data. It is wrong data that does not announce itself.

  • Stale state that looks current
  • Silent write failures (catch(e){})
  • A field that accepts your typing and discards it
  • Derived values presented as if they were entered

Each of these produces a number you would act on with full confidence. Instinct to preserve: catching stale entries, future-dated records, wrong resolved dates, negative reconciliations. That instinct has caught real errors repeatedly.
